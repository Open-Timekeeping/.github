# Code of Conduct

We want Open Timekeeping to be a project where technical work gets done well and where people enjoy doing it. That takes some baseline expectations of how we treat each other. This document spells them out plainly.

## What we expect

- **Be direct and technical.** Disagreement on technical questions is healthy and welcome. Argue with the substance, not the person.
- **Assume good faith.** Most disagreements come from differing context, not bad intent. Ask before accusing.
- **Be patient with people who are learning.** A short explanation costs less than a closed PR. We were all new to something once.
- **Credit work honestly.** If you build on someone's idea or code, say so.
- **Communicate in English in the project's public spaces** (issues, PRs, discussions). That is the working language of the project. Side-channels can be whatever you want.

## What is not okay

- Personal attacks, insults, demeaning language, or sustained hostility toward another contributor.
- Harassment of any kind, including unwanted contact after being asked to stop.
- Discriminatory remarks targeting people on the basis of who they are, race, ethnicity, nationality, sex, gender identity or expression, sexual orientation, disability, religion, age, or any similar characteristic.
- Threats, doxxing, or anything that puts another contributor at risk.
- Persistently derailing technical discussions to relitigate already-decided points.
- Bringing unrelated political fights into the project.

This is not exhaustive. If something looks like a problem, it probably is.

## Scope

This applies anywhere the project operates: GitHub issues and PRs, the project hub, any official chat the project hosts, and conferences or events where you are representing Open Timekeeping. It does not police what contributors do in their personal lives.

## Reporting

If you see a problem or are subject to one, email **jeremy@willemse.co**. Reports go to the maintainer. The substance of a report is confidential by default; the maintainer will discuss it with the person involved only to the extent needed to act on it, and with your knowledge.

If the concern is about the maintainer themselves, escalate to someone you trust who can engage on your behalf. We are a small project and we will figure out a fair process.

## What happens after a report

The maintainer will:

1. Acknowledge the report within a few days.
2. Look into it, read the context, talk to people, form a view.
3. Decide on an action, anywhere from "no action, this was a misread" through a private conversation, a warning, a temporary ban from project spaces, or a permanent ban.
4. Tell you what was decided. The decision is the maintainer's. People are welcome to disagree publicly; the decision stands until reversed.

We are not going to run a tribunal. The point is to keep the project a good place to work.

## Good-faith protection

Reporting a concern in good faith, even one that turns out to be a misread, will not be held against you. Knowingly fabricated reports are themselves a violation.
